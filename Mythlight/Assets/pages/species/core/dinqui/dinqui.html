Dinqui

Protectors of the Ancient

Overview

Created by the immortals, the Dinqui were given a singular purpose: to guard the Kawanot cities of the mortal plane. Obedient and ever watchful, they remained at their posts for millennia.

They coexisted first with the Kawanot and later the Gorgon, living simple and purposeful lives. Viewing themselves as lesser than their creators never troubled them; instead, they took pride in fulfilling the role they were made for.

Only after the Fall of the North Star, when the cities they protected lost their purpose, did dissatisfaction begin to grow. Over generations, they became increasingly detached from their original duty. Eventually, most Dinqui families left the ruins behind, integrating into other societies, no longer believing their ancient cities would ever return to their former glory.

Appearance

Dinqui stand just over one meter tall, resembling living gargoyles sculpted from stone. Their bodies span a range of shades, from pearl white to deep coal black, though earthy tones reminiscent of natural elements are also common.
Their features are distinctly animalistic, both in face and limb. Their eyes are strikingly vibrant, far brighter than the rest of their bodies, and share their color with the inside of their mouths, their tongues, and even their scars.
Though fully bipedal, Dinqui naturally adopt a hunched posture, often keeping all four limbs close to the ground. It is far more common to see one crouched than standing upright.
They possess one to three pairs of wings, varying greatly in size, from nearly body-length to as short as a forearm. While many can glide, not all are capable of true flight. Despite their stone-like weight, a surprising number can lift themselves into the air.

Culture

Dinqui require remarkably little sustenance, even compared to similarly sized cold-blooded creatures. Hunger typically arises only when healing from injury or fighting off illness.

Dinqui enjoy their quiet time and the wind on their skin. Often preferring to sit atop rooftops than in the busy crowds below. The cities they occupy usually have reinforced public rooftops for this exact purpose. While “on guard," they often do not show signs of life, and because of their capability of sleeping without lying down, it is often difficult to tell if they are watching your every move or napping atop a rooftop. Dinqui are more often than not perfectly satisfied by mere observation of the life around them, deeming it productive. Though comfortable in solitude, Dinqui still form strong communal bonds.

Following the Fall of the North Star, and the abandonment of their original purpose, Dinqui turned toward creation rather than preservation. They began crafting, restoring, and maintaining what remained. Even after integrating into wider societies, this instinct endured. They place great value on objects, not for ownership or wealth, but for their history and craftsmanship. Restoration, upkeep, and preservation remain central to their identity. Many take pride in maintaining public spaces, practicing historical preservation, or quietly engaging in hobbies such as birdwatching.
