The Mythlight Rules

The complete reference for the 2d6 engine — how it resolves, how a character grows, how the fight unfolds, and how a Narrator holds it all together.

I
Chapter One

Core Mechanics

Mythlight is built to be simple and narrative-first — rules that are easy to carry to the table and that get out of the way of the story. Everything else in this book rests on the small handful of mechanics below.

What You Need

Dice
2 six-sided dice per player. No polyhedral dice are used anywhere in this system.
Character Sheet
Stats, traits, equipment, and backstory, all in one place.
One Narrator
Guides the story, plays every NPC, and presents the challenges.

The 2d6 Engine

Most actions resolve on a roll of 2d6 + Modifiers vs CS — you succeed if your total meets or beats the Challenge Score.

Advantage
Roll one additional d6 (3d6 total) and keep all of them. There is no upper limit on dice added this way.
Disadvantage
Remove one d6 (1d6 total). The pool may be reduced all the way to 0d6 — and you lose the ability to Midnight.
✦ Midnight — Critical Success

Triggered only on two natural sixes. The action automatically hits and deals double damage. With Advantage, the trigger stays the same, but every additional six rolled increases the effect further.

✷ Snake Eyes — Critical Failure

Triggered on two natural ones. The action automatically misses and causes a complication — tripping, self-damage, or a narrative setback. Snake Eyes cannot occur while rolling 0d6.

Grace

You gather Grace as you fail — a reserve to spend when the moment is dire.

Gain
+1 Grace on any failed roll.
Delay
Success doesn't reset Grace, but delays its growth — you must fail twice in a row to gain another point.
Spend
Spend all of your Grace at once to boost a single roll. Maximum Grace is 6.
Reset
Resets whenever combat starts or ends, or when you sleep.
II
Chapter Two

Character Creation

These rules govern how a character is built, and how every part of them functions at the table — from a first breath of attribute points to the trait that finally defines who they are.

Attributes — Point Buy

You have 3 points to distribute, with a maximum of 2 points in any single stat initially. Species further modify these.

Body
Physical strength, agility, endurance.
Skill
Reflexes, tinkering, fine manual skill.
Mind
Magical knowledge, control, potency.
Style
Conversation, persuasion, social understanding.

Species & Paths

Your character is more than numbers — it's also past, culture, and appearance. Use these to inform both roleplay and mechanical choices.

Species
Represent your character's physical traits and cultural background. Each grants minor bonuses to base attributes and one unique trait. You pick one at character creation.
Paths
Thematic collections of traits. You don't choose a Path directly — as you invest points into traits belonging to one, you progress along it automatically. Reaching certain thresholds unlocks Breakpoints: powerful passives or a +1 to a relevant attribute.

Feats

Each attribute governs a set of feats that can be enhanced over time. At character creation you increase 4 of these by 1. Feat checks always follow 2d6 + Attribute + Feat Modifier.

Body
Might — lifting, breaking, melee power.
Brawn — endurance, toughness, health.
Fortitude — resisting poison, pain, fatigue.
Skill
Finesse — dexterity, precision, ranged weapons.
Reflex — dodging, reacting quickly.
Tinker — crafting, repairing, modifying devices.
Mind
Arcane — magical knowledge and manipulation.
Spirit — mental and magical endurance.
Scholarship — research, history, learned knowledge.
Style
Speech — getting what you want by talking.
Insight — reading intentions and hidden truths.
Awareness — perception of surroundings and danger.

Health & Guard

Health
Scales with level. At level 1 you have 4 + Brawn health; each subsequent level grants 2 + Brawn.
Guard
Temporary health, always lost before Health. Guard cannot be healed once lost — only regained through traits or external effects.
Death
At 0 Health you fall unconscious. Only Final Blows can harm you further; you resist 4 Final Blows before dying, at the Narrator's discretion.

Rest (10+ minutes)

A rest is time spent on tasks that aren't physically demanding — up to 4 hours. For each hour rested, including the first 10 minutes, you regain health equal to your level, and regain any Active trait uses.

Sleep (8 hours)

  • Sleep at least 6 hours: fully recover health and mana — or reduce Fatigue by 1 if you have any.
  • Sleep at least 4 hours: recover half of your health and mana.
  • Sleep less than 4 hours: no recovery at all.
  • Any amount of sleep prevents Fatigue gain.

Fatigue imposes a stacking −2 penalty to all rolls per level. Reaching level 6 without sleep is fatal.

Traits & Leveling Up

There are no classes and no professions in Mythlight. Instead, every feature, ability, and bonus is acquired from a trait — gained as you level, varying in price and power, and the true core of a character's progression. Many traits belong to a Path; investing enough into one unlocks its Breakpoints. At level 1 you have 2 trait points to spend — choose wisely.

Leveling is dictated by the Narrator. Each level grants a trait point, and select levels also grant feat points — spendable only once per feat, so 2 feat points must raise 2 different feats. Characters run from level 1 to level 12.

Spellcasting

A core part of the system — mana is spent to cast spells you know. Spellcasting requires a trait that grants it, such as Arcane Student, and no character can have more than one spellschool unlocked. If you intend to play a caster, read the dedicated Spells page in full.

Equipment & Encumbrance

Armor
Base armor is 4 + Fortitude, with worn armor granting a flat bonus. Shields 1–2, Light 1–2, Medium 2–3, Heavy 4–5. One piece per slot unless a trait says otherwise.
Weaponry
You may wield as many weapons as you have arms, but without traits only 1 weapon is fully effective. Might and Finesse set your maximum proficiency investment in Melee, Ranged, and Finesse weapons.

Encumbrance Limit = Level + Might + Brawn — item weights run None (0), Light (1), Medium (2), Heavy (3), Cumbersome (4); 100 coins equal 1 weight.

StateEffect
Non-encumbered < LimitBusiness as usual.
Encumbered > LimitMovement halved; 0 movement imposes disadvantage.
Overencumbered > 2× LimitMovement halved, and disadvantage on all Body/Skill checks.
Crushed > 3× LimitMovement 0, disadvantage on all checks, 1 damage every 10 minutes.

Chapter Three

Combat Rules

These rules govern combat and all of its sub-systems — from the tile you stand on to the condition that's about to expire.

Movement

Combat takes place on a grid-based map. Each tile equals 1 unit of Speed. Movement is limited to tiles directly adjacent to one another — diagonal movement never skips a tile.

A Dash ignores opportunity attacks and terrain penalties, but is never available without a strictly declared source. Jumping is an alternative to normal movement, useful for avoiding Rough or Slippery terrain or clearing hazards — its maximum distance is half your Speed (rounded up), and you cannot jump through an occupied space.

TerrainEffect
FirmNo movement penalties.
RoughEach tile of movement costs 2 units of movement.
SlipperyYou may only move in one direction at a time; each turn prompts a Reflex check.
DarkNo light — you are considered Blinded.
DimVisibility reduced; sight-based Awareness checks suffer disadvantage.
VisibleLighting is clear; creatures and objects are seen normally.
BlindingLight is overwhelming — you automatically fail sight-based checks.

Map Sizes & Grappling

Size defines a creature's "hitbox," affecting adjacency and area effects. Creatures generally cannot occupy the same space.

Tiny · 1×1
Can occupy the space of medium creatures and above with no penalty.
Small · 1×1
Can occupy the space of large creatures and above with no penalty.
Medium · 1×1
The standard size for most creatures and player characters.
Large · 2×2 / Huge · 3×3
Larger creatures and true titans; anything bigger should be assembled from multiple size categories (a huge body, large wings, a medium head).

To grapple, both creatures roll a contesting Might check; success applies Restrained to both until one lets go or breaks free as an action. Grappling something larger than you imposes 1 disadvantage per size class above yours; grappling something smaller grants 1 advantage per size class below.

A Radius begins as a 3×3 square (1 unit radius) and expands outward by one tile per unit. A Cone is a triangle of tiles whose width equals half its height, rounded down, always centered on the caster.

Ranged Combat & Cover

  • Any creature within range can be shot at; attacks beyond range automatically fail.
  • Ranged attacks made while adjacent to an enemy suffer disadvantage.
  • Creatures within a 1-unit radius of a target — including diagonals — count as adjacent, and count as Partial (Reinforced) Cover for anyone shooting past them.
Partial Cover
Flimsy, Sturdy, or Reinforced — imposes a −1 to −3 penalty depending on material.
Half Cover
Imposes disadvantage on the attack roll.
Full Cover
The target cannot be targeted at all, barring traits that bypass it.

Turn Structure & Action Economy

Scene
One full cycle of every creature's turn. Unconscious creatures drop to the bottom of the order until revived.
Ambush
When combat begins against an unaware target, the initiator acts before normal initiative or turn delay.
Opportunity Attack
When a creature leaves another's melee range, the remaining creature may make a melee attack against it.
NPC Initiative
NPCs act on a Turn Delay set by when they joined combat, specified on their stat block.

Each turn grants 2 Actions, with only 1 Attack per turn by default. Each Scene grants 1 Reaction. A Held Action may be kept until your next turn and triggered when a declared condition is met.

Attack TypeFormula
Melee Attack2d6 + Proficiency + Weapon Mod + Accuracy
Finesse Attack2d6 + Proficiency + Weapon Mod + Accuracy
Firearm Attack2d6 + Proficiency + Weapon Mod + Accuracy
Magic Attack2d6 + Arcane Total + Focus Mod + Accuracy

Off-hand attacks use only the base stat score. A weapon with 0 Proficiency is treated as Improvised — improvised weapons use only Might (melee) or Finesse (thrown).

Companions

Companions fight, assist, or travel alongside a character. They exist to support, not replace, player agency.

Health
Crafted companions: Health equals their Durability. Tamed companions: Health equals the max on their original stat block.
Armor
Fixed at 6 + ½ Level (rounded down), before equipment or trait bonuses.
Attributes
Only Body, Mind, and Skill. Crafted companions default to 2 in each unless stated otherwise; tamed companions use their original stat block.

Companions have no Feats, Grace, or Mana unless explicitly stated. At 0 Health a companion is incapacitated, with 1 Final Blow resistance per Sleep — Narrators are encouraged to treat companion deaths narratively, the same weight as a player's. They act immediately after their controller, with 2 Actions, 1 Attack, and 1 Reaction per Scene. By default only one active combat companion is allowed per character, unless a trait says otherwise.

A companion only performs complex actions when commanded: a full Command (1 controller action) to Attack, Move, Interact, Defend, or use a Special ability, or a free Simple Direction to Follow, Stay, or Retreat.

Conditions

Conditions represent ongoing states — some stack, others simply persist until removed. Multiple stacks can be removed at once, and removal generally requires a check, item, magic, or rest.

Poison
Stacks. Each scene one stack expires, dealing 1 Poison damage while active.
Mantra
Stacks. Each turn heal 1 and lose 1 stack; healing increases every 4 stacks.
Bleed
Stacks. Each stack reduces healing by 1; one stack expires per scene.
Boon
Stacks. The next healing, Guard, or Armor gain is increased by your stacks, then all are consumed.
Slow
Stacks. Lose 1 movement per stack and cannot react; expires next scene if not reapplied.
Speed
Stacks. Gain 1 movement per stack and may take 2 reactions; expires next scene if not reapplied.
Stunned
Stacks. Each stack removes 1 action; at 0 actions your turn is skipped.
Haste
Stacks. Each stack grants an additional action; expires at end of scene if not reapplied.
Prone
Movement becomes 0, non-ranged attacks against you gain advantage, standing costs 1 action, and you cannot dash.
Flying
Immune to floor hazards; move up to Movement in any direction at a fixed height.
Restrained
Movement 0, attacks against you gain advantage, you cannot attack. Ending usually requires a Might check.
Ethereal
Pass through creatures and obstacles; cannot be grappled or harmed by non-magical effects.
Exhausted
Stacks. Each grants 1 temporary Fatigue (never fatal this way); one stack expires per scene.
Invigorated
Stacks. Each removes 1 Fatigue, to a minimum of 0; one stack expires per scene.
Weakened
Stacks. Each reduces damage dealt by 1; an attack reduced to 0 damage cannot be made. Expires next scene.
Astute
Stacks. Reduces Midnight roll requirements by 1 per stack.
Blinded
Disadvantage on your attacks; attacks against you gain advantage.
Vigil
Advantage on your attacks; attacks against you suffer disadvantage.
Taunt
Your next attack, including opportunity attacks, must target the taunting creature; expires once made.
Charmed
You must follow the charmer's commands (never harmful ones); expires at end of scene if not reapplied.
Burn
Take 1 damage each turn; expires at end of scene if not reapplied.
Chilled
−2 to Reflex; expires at the end of your next turn if not reapplied.
Compromised
Stacks. Each reduces Armor by 1; at 0 Armor all attacks auto-hit. One stack expires per scene.
Bolstered
Stacks. Gain 1 Armor per stack; one stack expires each time you take damage.
Madness
Casting a spell requires rolling 1d6 and paying that result as the mana cost instead.
Focus
Stacks. Spells cost 1 less mana per stack of Focus.
Intoxicated
−2 Finesse and −2 Scholarship.
Stoned
−2 Reflex and −2 Insight.
Feared
Must use at least one movement action per turn to flee the source of fear.
Courage
Stacks. +1 to resisting Intimidation and Fear; a stack expires each time you resist such an effect.
Unconscious
Cannot act; taking damage costs 1 Final Blow Resistance.
Resolve
Stacks. If you'd fall unconscious, consume 1 Final Blow Resistance and 1 stack instead; at 0 Health from this, remain at 1.
Dazed
Active trait uses are disabled while any stacks remain; one decays at end of turn.
Vigor
Stacks. The next trait Use that would be consumed instead removes 1 stack of Vigor.
Silenced
Cannot cast spells while any stacks remain; one decays at end of turn.
Hesitation
Cannot make unarmed or weapon attacks while any stacks remain; one decays at end of turn.
Rage
Lose 6 Armor, gain +1 Damage and +2 Accuracy; dismissed after 2 scenes without attacking or being hit.
Pacifism
Cannot take any action that would directly harm another creature.
Sleep
Cannot act; regain 1 health per scene slept. Any damage taken causes Awakening.
Alert
Cannot be Ambushed, Surprised, or fall asleep involuntarily; you may still take rests.

Chapter Four

Mounts & Vehicle Combat

Trusted steeds and fancy rides — faster and stronger than two feet. Here's how they carry you through a fight.

Mounted Combat

Any creature capable of carrying a passenger can serve as a mount; medium vehicles count as mounts too, while large vehicles use full vehicle rules. While mounted, rider and mount share a tile and an initiative slot, and the rider uses the mount's Movement instead of their own. Mounting and dismounting each cost 1 Action.

Melee attacks against a mounted target suffer an accuracy penalty equal to the rider's Reflex, and an attacker must declare whether they're targeting the rider or the mount. Area effects hit both; floor hazards affect only the mount. Opportunity Attacks normally hit the mount, though attackers with Reach may target the rider instead.

StatUnmountedMounted
HealthHealth, replaced by Morale while mounted
Armor6 + Mount's Body Score6 + Rider's Reflex
MovementMovement

Morale is set to the mount's current Health at the moment it's mounted; whenever the rider takes damage, the mount loses Morale equal to half that damage, and a directly-targeted mount takes full Morale damage. A rider may spend 1 Action to Soothe their mount, restoring Morale equal to half the rider's level.

When Morale hits zero, the mount attempts to forcefully dismount its rider. On a success it recovers Morale equal to the rider's Reflex; on a failure the rider is dismounted, knocked Prone, and placed in an adjacent tile — the mount becomes Feared of whoever caused the loss and flees, obeying only Hide or Seek Cover commands.

Vehicle mounts use a fixed stat block that never changes while mounted and don't track Morale — they lose Durability only when targeted directly, hit by floor hazards, or drained by traits. An unridden vehicle is non-functional and acts as a combat obstacle.

Vehicle Combat

Medium
Motorcycle, quadbike. Seats 1 (up to 2). 1×1 tile. Hits land on the rider, rolled against their armor.
Big
Car, wagon. Seats 4 (up to 5), 1 modification slot, 4×4 tiles, 13 Armor. Hits land on any rider, rolled against the vehicle's armor.
Large
Tank, truck, train, larger ships. 5+ occupants, 2 modification slots, 6+ tiles (min. width 2), 17 Armor. Hits land on any visible rider, rolled against the vehicle's armor.

Movement: Medium 6 tiles, Big 10 tiles, Large 8 tiles. Cover: Medium grants Partial Cover, Big grants Half Cover and can block line of sight, Large grants Full (and sometimes Total) Cover. Basic repairs restore Durability at roughly 1 hour per 10 points, typically needing a successful Crafting or Engineering check; advanced repairs may need specific parts or magical assistance.

ModificationEffect

Bigger Fuel TankExtends the fuel pool, reducing consumption.

Chapter Five

Narrator Rules

You lead the story, control its forces, and guide the flow of events while your players shape the outcome through their choices. You hold final authority on rules and rulings — and on the table's tone, fairness, and safety.

Overview & System Familiarity

Conflict between characters can enrich a story; conflict between players does not — protect the table's enjoyment and keep the experience collaborative. Mythlight is designed to be modified: adjust, remove, or expand rules as your story needs. The system is a framework, and you are its interpreter. Familiarize yourself with the core mechanics before running a session, but you're not expected to memorize everything — checking mid-session is fine, and players should know their own abilities.

Starting Resources & Tools

Decide what characters begin with — early equipment shapes the tone and difficulty of the first sessions. Combine or choose between Starting Funds (an appropriate sum, spent freely within reason) and Starting Gear (a predefined loadout: light or medium armor, one proficient weapon, modest funds, essential supplies). Ground equipment in the narrative — consider how each character acquired their items.

Tools enhance specific feats and are defined by purpose rather than base feat (Brewing Tools, Crafting Tools). They typically grant a +1 to +3 bonus when appropriately applied; characters gain proficiencies through traits, or may start play proficient at your discretion.

Creature Taming

Anyone can attempt to tame a creature if they pass the necessary checks, though using a tamed creature in combat is locked behind traits like Beast Whisperer. Different creatures need different prerequisites — a squirrel wants nuts and a decent Speech/Awareness roll, a bear could get you mauled. Not everything is tameable; wild beasts avoid or attack, and even purchased animals can escape or refuse to listen.

Taming requires repeated attempts, each costing 1–4 weight units of appropriate food (scaling with size) and a check that drops one difficulty tier per success. Purchased animals start between Difficult and Standard; wild animals always start at Hard.

The Effect Stack

Multiple effects may modify the same stat at once. To avoid confusion, all modifiers resolve in a fixed order:

OrderModifier TypeExamples
1SpeciesInherent stat modifiers and traits from a character's species.
2BreakpointsPassive bonuses and attribute increases unlocked by Path investment.
3TraitsCharacter traits providing bonuses or penalties.
4Spells / Items / CraftsTemporary effects from magic, equipment, or crafted items.
5Conditions / HazardsOngoing effects from conditions or the environment.

Example — Movement, base 5: a species trait grants +1 (5→6); a Breakpoint grants +1 (6→7); traits reduce it by 2 (7→5); boots grant +3 (5→8); rough terrain then halves the fully-modified value on the move itself. In simple cases the order may not matter, but in complex interactions, sequencing keeps things balanced.

CS Difficulty & Social Interactions

Whenever an action's outcome is uncertain, assign a CS (Challenge Score) reflecting its difficulty — this applies to physical feats, knowledge, environment, and social encounters alike.

DifficultyCS RangeDescription
Trivial4 (±2)Easily accomplished by anyone with basic competence.
Easy6 (±2)Requires some effort or skill, generally achievable.
Standard8 (±2)A reasonable challenge for a competent character.
Difficult10 (±2)Requires significant skill, preparation, or luck.
Hard12 (±2)A formidable challenge needing multiple attempts or creative solutions.

The ±2 range lets you adjust within a tier for context, preparation, tools, or narrative positioning. Impossible actions simply aren't assigned a CS — you're never required to give a chance at something that cannot reasonably occur.

Social encounters should default to roleplay first; roll only when the outcome is genuinely uncertain or contested. When a roll is needed, use the same tiers with CS + Target Insight ± Reputation Modifier — positive reputation may lower the effective difficulty, hostility or distrust may raise it. Adjust freely for tone and stakes.

Campaign & Session Preparation

Before the first session, establish the world's stability, magic presence, and political tensions; clarify tone and player-safety expectations; give every character a reason to exist within the world and party; and introduce a central conflict, threat, or mystery to drive early engagement.

1. NPCs
Keep a quick-reference list of names, traits, quirks, and goals for on-the-spot creation.
2. Quests
Prepare small jobs and at least one main questline, and connect them together where possible.
3. Points of Interest
Prepare at least as many POIs as there are players for any new location, including expected sites like shops and taverns.
4. Loot & Inventory
Rollable tables for chests, and prepared price lists for stores — empty chests and empty shelves weaken immersion.
5. Random Events
Themed tables for travel and rest, refreshed periodically; keep highly lethal outcomes out of general travel unless the region calls for it.
6. Improvisation
No amount of prep accounts for every decision — preparation supports the campaign, but flexibility sustains it.

Languages can be added freely to reflect your setting — not every settlement speaks the same tongue, and private languages can create secrecy between factions or party members, used intentionally rather than as frustration.

Each session should stay open-ended: a clear Primary Objective, optional Secondary Hooks, NPC motivations, relevant stat blocks and CS values prepared in advance, anticipated player resources, and the flexibility to pivot. Let consequences emerge from player action — the world reacts, it does not revolve.

Downtime & Travel

1. Potions & Crafting
Guaranteed success if the character commits hours equal to the item's CS requirement — no roll needed. Discovering new potion recipes needs a Hard experimentation check per ingredient; crafting recipes must be learned, never discovered this way.
2. Spells
Learned from scrolls or spellbooks in 4 hours per spell level (the level must already be unlocked, and enough mana to cast it must be available). Cantrips take 2 hours plus a successful Difficult Arcana check.
3. Traits
Rare training manuals allow new traits to be learned — 10 days per trait point, with all normal prerequisites still required.
4. Proficiencies
4 hours a day for 7 consecutive days increases a weapon proficiency by 1 point.

Languages: 3 hours a day for one month raises Language Score by 1 (1→6); at 6 the character is fluent. The Narrator holds final authority over all downtime limits and time requirements.

Combat-Relevant NPC Creation

Combat-ready NPCs need a structured stat block to function within initiative, damage resolution, and the Effect Stack.

StatHow to Set It
MovementFollows normal player movement rules. Average ranges: Slow ~3, Moderate ~5, High ~7.
HealthCalculate total party Health [H], then pick a pool: Easy 0.5×H, Moderate 1×H, Hard 2×H — distributed evenly among combatants (allies get equal HP but don't count toward the division).
ArmorSelected from the CS difficulty scale according to the desired encounter challenge.
Turn DelayFixed (acts after X player turns at combat start) or Recursive (acts every X player turns).
AttributesBody (Melee), Skill (Ranged), Mind (Spells) in place of feats. Distribute 4 points total; +2 for a primary/boss target, −2 for a weak/swarm enemy.
Weakness / Resistance / ImmunityWeakness: 50% more damage from a type. Resistance: 50% less. Immunity: unaffected entirely.
Abilities & TraitsAssign traits totaling 0.5× the NPC's Health in trait points; if a trait requires Level, use the NPC's highest Attribute score instead.
SpellcastingAtk Bonus = Mind, Dmg Bonus = Mind ÷ 2, Spell CS = 5 + Mind, and 0.5× Health worth of spells (cantrips included).

Chapter Six

One Shot Character Creation

One shot characters are quick to build and quick to play. The focus is flavor, not optimization — aim for a session of about 3 to 4 hours.

1. Choose a Species
Pick a single species and use only its Attribute bonus. Species also provides your base movement, a trait, and other minor statistics.
2. Assign Traits
2 starting Trait Points, plus +1 per character level. For fast play, the Random Trait Generator is strongly recommended.
3. Attributes & Feats
No Feats are used — base Attributes apply directly to rolls. You have 4 attribute points, maximum 2 in any single Attribute.
4. Equipment
Choose one appropriate weapon and one armor type (or none). Other gear is optional. Unarmored: Armor = 6 + Body.
5. Spellcasting
Only spellcasters track Mana, gaining 1 per level. Cantrips cost none; casting at a higher level costs 1 extra mana per level. Half your mana restores on Rest, all of it on Sleep.

Health, Guard & Defeat

Health
6 + Brawn at level 1; +2 + Brawn each level after.
Guard
Temporary health, always lost first, and cannot be healed.
Unconscious & Final Blows
At 0 Health you're unconscious and cannot act; resist 4 Final Blows per rest before dying.

Resting heals Health equal to your level per hour; sleeping fully restores Health and Mana.

Core Mechanics, Turns & Range

RollResultOutcome

< CSFailure
Two natural 6sMidnight — automatic success, double damage
Two natural 1sSnake Eyes — automatic failure, complication

Initiative is determined by word of mouth; non-players use a turn delay. Becoming unconscious drops you to the bottom of the order, and mounts/companions share their controller's turn. Actions: 2 per turn, 1 attack per turn, 1 reaction per scene — Move, Attack, Cast, Interact, and Command all count as common actions.

Combat uses a square grid; every character can move up to 5 tiles per action, only to tiles that are touching. Leaving melee range triggers Opportunity Attacks, which consume the attacker's Reaction. For ranged attacks: targets beyond max range can't be hit, attacking while an enemy is in melee range suffers disadvantage, Partial Cover imposes −2 Accuracy, and Full Cover prevents targeting entirely. Line of sight is measured tile-center to tile-center.

Companions act immediately after their controller, with 2 Actions and 1 Attack, no Feats, Mana, or Grace, and only one combat companion per player.

Narrator Guidance for One Shots

Default CS
6 Easy, 8 Standard, 12 Hard, 16 "Impossible."
NPC Health
0.5× party Health total = Easy, 1× = Standard, 2× = Hard — divided among all NPCs present in a multi-NPC encounter.
NPC Traits
Between 1 and 4 traits, scaled to how difficult the NPC should be to defeat.
